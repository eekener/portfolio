A coach or consultant's website has a different job than most business sites. You're not running an e-commerce store. You're selling expertise, trust, and a transformation — and those things are harder to convey than a product with specs and photos.

WordPress is an excellent platform for coaches and consultants, but what makes a great coaching website is different from what makes a great restaurant site or SaaS landing page. This guide covers exactly what you need (and don't need) on your WordPress site to attract and convert clients.

## The Goal: Convert Visitors Into Discovery Calls

Everything on your coaching or consulting website should serve one of two purposes:
1. Help the right visitors understand you're the right person to help them
2. Make it easy for them to take the next step (usually booking a call)

Visitors who find your site are often comparison-shopping. They've searched for someone who helps with their specific problem, and they're evaluating whether you understand their situation and whether they trust you. Your site needs to speak directly to that.

## The Pages You Actually Need

### Homepage

Your homepage has roughly 5 seconds to communicate: who you help, what problem you solve, and why you're credible. The most effective coaching homepages follow a clear structure:

**1. Hero section** — A clear statement of who you help and the transformation you offer. "I help mid-career professionals land senior leadership roles in 90 days" is infinitely better than "Welcome to my coaching practice."

Your hero should include a professional photo — people are hiring you as a person. Faceless brand homepages don't convert well for personal services.

**2. Who it's for** — A short section helping visitors qualify themselves: "This is for you if..." or "I work with..." Many coaches are afraid to exclude anyone; this actually reduces conversions because vague positioning attracts nobody.

**3. The transformation** — What does life look like after working with you? Focus on outcomes, not process. Clients don't buy sessions or methodologies; they buy results.

**4. Social proof** — Testimonials, client logos, media mentions, credentials. Position these early and return to them throughout the page.

**5. A clear call to action** — "Book a free discovery call" or similar. Make it the easiest thing to do on the page.

### About Page

Your about page is typically the second most visited page on a coaching website. Visitors who are considering working with you want to know your story — how you came to do this work, what your credentials are, and whether you feel like a real person.

What works on a coaching about page:
- Your personal story, particularly your own struggle and transformation related to your niche
- Your credentials and approach (methodology, certifications, years of experience)
- Multiple professional photos — in different contexts, not just a headshot
- A clear statement of who you serve and why
- A call to action at the bottom

What to avoid:
- A sterile professional biography that reads like a LinkedIn summary
- A generic "my passion is helping people" opener
- No photos, or only a single small headshot

### Services/Work With Me Page

This page needs to do two things: explain clearly what you offer and give enough detail for the right person to say "yes, this is for me."

Structure for each service or offer:
- What it is (name and brief description)
- Who it's for (be specific)
- What's included (format, frequency, duration)
- The outcome/result clients typically experience
- Investment (pricing)
- How to get started (link to application or booking)

Coaches often fear showing prices on their website. The research is clear: showing pricing reduces tire-kicker inquiries and increases conversion rate among genuinely qualified prospects. At minimum, show a starting price or a range.

### Testimonials / Results Page

Social proof is arguably the most important conversion element for a coaching website. Collect and display as many genuine client testimonials as you can, including:

- Written testimonials with client name, title, and optionally a photo
- Specific results (numbers, timeframes, specific outcomes when clients will share them)
- Video testimonials (convert significantly better than text — even short, informal ones)
- Before/after case studies if your work produces measurable results

Sprinkle testimonials throughout your site, not just on a dedicated page. The decision page (your services or homepage) should have testimonials adjacent to the call to action.

### Blog or Resources Section

A blog serves coaches and consultants well for SEO and authority building — but only if you'll actually maintain it. A blog with three posts from 2022 is worse than no blog at all.

If you're going to blog, focus on content that:
- Targets keywords your ideal clients search for
- Demonstrates your expertise and perspective specifically
- Provides genuine value rather than generic advice

One well-researched post per month, written consistently over 12–18 months, is more valuable than sporadic high-output periods followed by silence.

### Contact and Booking

Make it easy to take the next step. For most coaches and consultants, the primary CTA is scheduling a discovery call. Set up an online booking system integrated with your calendar:

- **Calendly** — simple, widely used, free tier available
- **Acuity Scheduling** — more powerful, better client experience
- **TidyCal** — lower cost alternative with solid features

Embed the booking page directly on your WordPress site rather than redirecting to an external URL. Keeping visitors on your site maintains trust and reduces drop-off.

Also set up a simple contact form for inquiries that don't fit the booking flow.

## WordPress Features Particularly Useful for Coaches

### Email List Building

An email list is your most reliable marketing asset — unlike social media, you own it. Integrate an email marketing platform (ConvertKit, ActiveCampaign, Mailchimp) with a WordPress opt-in form.

Common lead magnet ideas for coaches: a free guide, a quiz with results, a mini-training video, a checklist, or a free chapter from your book.

### Online Courses

If you want to scale beyond 1:1 work with online courses, WordPress handles this well. Options:
- **LearnDash** or **LifterLMS** — full-featured course platforms that run on WordPress
- **MemberPress** — flexible membership and course platform
- **Teachable or Kajabi** — not WordPress-based, but easier to set up if technology isn't your strength

For coaches just starting with digital products, a simple WooCommerce setup to sell a PDF guide or a video series is faster to deploy than a full course platform.

### Podcast Integration

If you have a podcast, WordPress integrates well with podcast hosting via Powerpress or Seriously Simple Podcasting. Embedding episodes on relevant blog posts and your homepage adds depth and builds the know/like/trust relationship with potential clients.

## What to Skip

**Complex navigation with 10+ menu items** — Your navigation should be: Home, About, Services, Blog (optional), Contact/Book a Call. That's it.

**Stock photos of faceless business people** — Coaching is personal. Real photos of you, your clients (with permission), or your actual working environment are worth infinitely more.

**A splash/intro page** — Get visitors to content immediately.

**Automating everything before you've manually validated your offer** — Build a lean, fast site first. Add automation and complexity only after you've proven clients convert through the basic flow.

A well-built WordPress coaching site doesn't need to be complex. It needs to clearly communicate who you help, demonstrate you understand their problem, build trust through social proof, and make the next step frictionless. Get those four things right and the site does its job.
